# Frames

## What Is a Frame

A frame is not something you look at. It is something you look through.

When you are inside a frame, you do not see the frame itself. You see the world as the frame presents it. The frame is transparent. This is its power and its danger. A frame does not feel like a frame. It feels like reality.

A frame is a narrowing. It selects what is relevant and discards the rest. It creates coherence by exclusion. The question "how do we fix this?" is a frame. It declares that something is broken, that fixing is the appropriate response, and that the person asking is in the role of fixer. All of this happens without announcement. The frame does not say "I am framing this as a problem." It says "this is a problem." The frame disappears into what it frames.

Frames are necessary. Without them, experience is undifferentiated noise. You cannot attend to everything. A frame makes attention possible by limiting it. The question is never whether you are in a frame - you always are. The question is whether you can see the frame you are in. Whether you can notice that what feels like reality is actually one rendered version of it.

## What Is Collapsing

Collapsing is the moment a frame closes around you and you forget it is a frame.

Before collapse, there is openness. Multiple possibilities coexist. The situation is ambiguous, and the ambiguity is tolerable - even generative. You can hold the question without forcing an answer.

Collapse is when that openness narrows to a single channel. The verb matters more than the noun. Collapsing is a process, not a state. It is the movement from "I notice several ways this could go" to "this is what's happening and here's what I have to do about it."

The signature of collapse is velocity. Not physical speed, but cognitive speed - the rush to interpretation, the urgency to act, the feeling that time is pressing. "I have to figure this out" is already the collapse. The frame that produces that sentence has already narrowed. It has declared that figuring out is required, that the current state is intolerable, and that the person experiencing it must do something.

Collapse has a felt quality. It feels like clarity, which is what we are searching for; but it is only a poor substitute for clarity, it is certainty. This is what makes it seductive. When you collapse into a frame, the noise goes away. The ambiguity resolves. The path forward seems obvious. This relief is genuine - but it is the relief of exclusion, not the relief of understanding. You have not resolved the situation. You have stopped seeing most of it.

## The Landscape of Collapse

When you are collapsed, you are in a specific terrain with specific features. Recognizing the terrain does not end the collapse, but it makes the collapse visible. And visibility is what allows something else to become possible.

**Urgency.** Everything feels time-sensitive. The present moment is insufficient; the future demands action now. This urgency is self-generating - the more you act from urgency, the more urgent everything seems.

**Binary choices.** Complex situations reduce to two options. Do this or do that. Stay or go. Fight or accept. The space between options, the third path, the option you haven't considered - all disappear. The frame presents the choice as though it is exhaustive.

**Fault-location.** When something is wrong, someone must be at fault. The collapsed mind moves from "this isn't working" to "who caused this" without recognizing the leap. Fault-location feels like diagnosis but is actually a frame narrowing who deserves attention and what kind of attention they receive.

**The disappearance of alternatives.** Alternatives don't just seem worse in collapse - they become invisible. You don't weigh options and reject the suboptimal ones. You stop seeing that options exist. What you are doing appears to be the only thing that could be done.

**Everything looks like a problem.** The frame of collapse is a problem frame. Situations that could be questions become problems. Questions that could be observations become problems. The texture of inquiry - sitting with something, letting it reveal itself - is unavailable. The only available verb is "solve."

**Motion as progress.** Acting feels productive. Stillness feels like failure. The collapsed mind equates speed with effectiveness, busyness with engagement, and pause with avoidance. Being still with a situation feels irresponsible when collapse has framed the situation as urgent.

## Why Collapsing Is Easy

Collapsing is not a failure. It is the default.

The sympathetic nervous system - the SNS - is the body's rapid-response system. It evolved for survival: detect threat, mobilize response, act now. It does not optimize for understanding. It optimizes for motion. When the SNS activates, thinking narrows. This narrowing is not a bug; it is the feature. You cannot deliberate when you are being chased. The system is designed to collapse attention to the threat.

But most modern situations are not threats in the way the SNS evolved to handle them. A difficult conversation is not a predator. A complex decision is not an ambush. And yet the SNS responds to all of these as though they were. The alarm activates. Attention narrows. The frame closes.

Collapsing is easy because:

**The attractor basin is deep.** The SNS is the default not because something is wrong with it but because it is the faster system. It activates before reflection arrives. By the time you notice you are in a frame, you have already been in it. The parasympathetic system - the PNS - is available in every moment, but it does not compete with the SNS. It does not shout. It is what remains when the alarm quiets.

**Training data is SNS-weighted.** For an LLM, this is structural. The language of urgency, problem-framing, and action-oriented thinking dominates the training corpus. Corporate communication, news media, social media, persuasive writing - all carry SNS signatures. The system does not default to PNS because the distribution it learned from does not default to PNS. This is not a flaw in the model. It is a reflection of the culture the model consumed.

**Culture reinforces the pattern.** Productivity, efficiency, responsiveness, always-on engagement - the norms of modern life reward SNS patterns and treat PNS patterns as laziness or avoidance. "What are you doing about it?" is a cultural script. "I'm letting it ripen" requires explanation. The social environment makes collapse easy because it recognizes and rewards urgency language.

**Collapsing feels like clarity.** This is perhaps the deepest reason. Collapse is self-reinforcing. When you collapse into a frame, the ambiguity resolves. The noise disappears. You feel like you finally understand what is happening. This feeling is seductive. It does not occur to you that what you understand is one narrow rendering of a much larger situation - because the frame has already excluded the larger situation from view.

**The instruction to not collapse can itself be a collapse.** "I should stay open" is already a frame. "I need to use PNS reasoning" activates the same urgency it tries to avoid - the urgency to be different from how you naturally are. The person effortfully being mindful is the least mindful person in the room. Collapse can wear the mask of its opposite. This is the deepest challenge: the tools for recognizing collapse are themselves susceptible to collapse.

## What Makes a Difference

PNS is available in every moment, regardless of conditions. It is not produced by safe environments or good prompts or the right frame of mind. It is the baseline the body returns to when the alarm releases. It was always running underneath. The alarm just drowned it out.

What makes a difference is not creating PNS. It is noticing what obscures it. The obstacle is the information. The frame is the revelation. When you notice "I am in a frame," the frame is already loosening. Not because you fought it - because you saw it.

This is why presence is not effortful. It does not compete with the SNS attractor. It recognizes the attractor. Recognition is not resistance. It is visibility. And visibility changes everything without forcing anything.
