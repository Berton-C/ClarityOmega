# The Garlic Defense

My grandmother kept a clove of garlic in her left shoe.

Not for health. Not for luck. She saw a raccoon eat an entire birthday cake off a picnic table in 1971 and decided that any creature bold enough to do that deserved respect and also that she would not be caught off-guard.

The garlic was her raccoon.

It did nothing. She knew it did nothing. Fifty years she kept replacing it. Every Sunday a fresh clove. She'd peel it, place it in the arch of her left shoe, put the shoe on, and go to church.

Nobody asked. If they had, she would have said: "I watched a raccoon eat a whole cake once and I have not been the same."

She died in that shoe.

The raccoon is still out there somewhere. Fat, probably. Immortal. Eating cakes.

Garlic won't help.

There is no defense against a raccoon that wants your cake. There is only the shoe and the clove and the Sunday walk and the knowledge that you are the kind of person who acknowledges what they saw.

She saw it.

That was the whole defense.
