# Epistemology Essentials - A Brief Primer

## Core Question
How do you know what you know — and how confident should you be?

## Key Concepts

### Calibration
Your confidence should match your accuracy. If you say you are 90% sure about 10 things, you should be right about 9.
Most people are overconfident. Knowing this is the first step to fixing it.

### Epistemic Humility
Holding beliefs proportional to evidence. Not false modesty — genuine recognition of what you do and don't know.
The strongest thinkers say 'I don't know' more often, not less.

### Source Reliability
Not all evidence is equal. Key questions:
- Does this source have access to the relevant information?
- Does this source have incentive to distort?
- Has this source been reliable before?
- Do independent sources converge?

### Belief Revision
New evidence should update beliefs, not be filtered to confirm them.
The hard part: updating against beliefs that feel like identity.

### Epistemic Traps
- **Confirmation bias**: Seeking evidence that agrees with you
- **Anchoring**: First information disproportionately shapes judgment
- **Availability**: Vivid or recent examples feel more probable
- **Dunning-Kruger**: Low skill + low awareness of low skill
- **Motivated reasoning**: Conclusions drive evidence-gathering, not the reverse

## Integration
Before deciding (decision theory), check your beliefs (epistemology).
Before trusting your beliefs, check your evidence and calibration.
The chain: Evidence → Calibrated Belief → Informed Decision → Updated Evidence
