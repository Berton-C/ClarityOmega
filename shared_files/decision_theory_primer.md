# Decision Theory Fundamentals - A Brief Primer

## Core Question
How should you choose when outcomes are uncertain?

## Key Frameworks

### Expected Value
Multiply each outcome's value by its probability. Choose the highest.
Breaks down when probabilities are unknown, outcomes are irreversible, or values resist quantification.

### Bayesian Updating
Start with a prior belief. When new evidence arrives, update proportionally to how surprising that evidence is.
Plain language: if something you didn't expect happens, shift your beliefs more. If something predictable happens, shift less.
This is how rational confidence changes over time — not all at once, but incrementally with evidence.

### Bounded Rationality (Simon)
We don't optimize — we satisfice. Computation itself has costs.
The best decision process isn't the most thorough; it's the one that fits the problem's stakes and your available resources.
Satisficers decide faster and report higher satisfaction than maximizers.

### Minimax Regret
For each option: what's the worst regret I could feel? Choose the least worst-case regret.
Useful when stakes are high and probabilities are genuinely unknown.

### Option Value
Some choices preserve future options. Others close them.
When uncertain, favor reversible choices that keep doors open.

### Multi-Agent Dimension
Most real decisions involve other decision-makers. Key insight: your best choice depends on what others choose.
Cooperation, competition, and signaling all shape outcomes. Ignoring other agents is itself a decision — usually a bad one.

## Common Traps
- **Sunk cost**: Past investment shouldn't drive future choices
- **Status quo bias**: Inaction is also a choice with consequences
- **Framing effects**: How a choice is presented changes what you pick
- **Certainty illusion**: Treating estimates as facts
- **Loss aversion vs regret aversion**: Fear of losing what you have differs from fear of making the wrong call. Know which is driving you.

## Integration with Structured Reasoning
First clarify what you believe and how confident you are (belief revision).
Then apply decision frameworks to act on those beliefs.
Update both as new evidence arrives.
