# Systems Thinking - A Brief Primer

## Core Question
What happens when parts interact in ways the parts alone don't predict?

## Key Concepts

### Feedback Loops
- **Reinforcing**: More leads to more (compound interest, viral spread, skill development)
- **Balancing**: System resists change and returns to equilibrium (thermostat, hunger, market correction)
Most real systems have both running simultaneously.

### Emergence
System-level properties that no component possesses alone. Consciousness from neurons. Traffic jams from drivers. Culture from individuals.
You cannot understand emergence by studying parts in isolation.

### Leverage Points
Small changes in the right place produce large shifts. Meadows hierarchy (most to least powerful):
1. Paradigm the system operates under
2. Goals of the system
3. Rules and incentives
4. Information flows
5. Parameters and numbers
Most interventions target the weakest leverage points.

### Delays
Effects lag behind causes. This creates oscillation and overreaction.
Key discipline: when an intervention seems to fail, check whether you are seeing delay rather than failure.

### Boundaries Are Choices
Every system boundary is drawn by an observer. What you include and exclude shapes what you can see.

## Common Traps
- **Linear thinking in nonlinear systems**: Doubling input rarely doubles output
- **Event fixation**: Reacting to events instead of seeing patterns and structures
- **Fixing symptoms**: Interventions that relieve symptoms while strengthening the underlying problem
- **Success to the successful**: Early advantages compound, creating inequality that looks like merit

## Integration
Systems thinking pairs with epistemology (check your boundaries), decision theory (leverage points guide action), and belief revision (delays mean evidence arrives late).
